History

The company was founded in 1964 by student Phil Knight, a middle distance runner in a team at the University of Oregon, and his coach Bill Bowerman. At first, it was called Blue Ribbon Sports and specialized in ordering sneakers in Asian countries and then selling them on the American market. Having invested in the case of $ 500, Knight and Bowerman ordered 300 pairs of sneakers from the Japanese company Onitsuka Tiger (currently ASICS). The first self-developed product of the company was the Nike sneakers with a waffle-like sole made in 1971, the idea that Bowerman got from his wife, more precisely from her waffle iron. In 1974, Blue Ribbon Sports was officially renamed Nike, Inc.

The name comes from the name of the Greek goddess of victory, Nika, and not from the English word that would be read "Nike." Ignorance of this fact led to widespread mislabeling of Nike in the Russian-speaking environment, which was even used in the name of the company's official representative in Russia. On March 3, 2008, the company completed the acquisition of a 100% stake in Umbro PLC, a manufacturer of sportswear and footwear, for £ 290.5 million. In 2012, the company was sold twice cheaper than Iconix Brand Group.

Nike in Russia

Since 1993, the exclusive distributor of Nike in the Russian market was Delta-Sport, but in 2003 Nike decided to establish its subsidiary, Nike LLC, to independently promote the brand in the market , which is currently the official distributor and authorized the importer of the company .

Criticism

Nike has been criticized for contracting factories in countries such as China, Vietnam, Indonesia and Mexico. A group of activists of Vietnam Labor Watch recorded that the factories with which Nike collaborated violated the minimum wage and overtime laws of Vietnam in late 1996, although Nike claims to have abandoned this practice .The company has become the object of much criticism because of poor working conditions and cheap labor that is used abroad in free trade zones, where, basically, the company's products are made. One of the sources of criticism is the book by Canadian journalist Naomi Klein “No Logo. People vs. Brands, ”and several Michael Moore documentaries.
